Part E | Sellers

Part E is about how this market power changes the firm's decisions and how these decisions impact society. So far we've examined how to coordinate groups to minimize opportunity cost. In Part A we motivated this search using the realization that minimizing opportunity cost can make everyone better off simultaneously. In Part B we showed that in some cases markets can be an effective coordination device. In Part C we showed that externalities undermine the efficiency of markets but that we could correct this type of market failure using correctly calibrated Government Policies. In Part D we introduced game theory to help understand the strategic interaction that leads to market failure around Public Goods and Common Resources. We even developed some tools like voting and enforceable permits which can go a long way to minimizing market failure.

This course we charted through economic theory has relied on our loose models of why buyers and sellers choose to interact. In Part A we introduced buyers' preferences with the demand curve and sellers' costs with the supply curve. Before investigating where buyers' preferences come from, in Part E we make the turn to examine the seller's incentives.

To do this we first start with the same production function we used in the Four Quadrant PPF. We input labor and generate an output. Inputs are costly. As we increase inputs we increase output but we also increase cost. This relationship between quantity and costs gives us our core cost measures: Average Total Cost (ATC) and Marginal Cost (MC).

We assume that the seller's aim to maximize their Profits, simply Total Revenue minus Total Cost. And we found that the solution to the Firm's Profit Maximization Problem is to set quantity where MC = MR. The shape of MC depends on the shape of the production function. The shape of MR depends on how much power the firm exerts over the market. Part E is about how this market power changes the firm's decisions and how these decisions impact society.
